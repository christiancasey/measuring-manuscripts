## Tractable, not just interesting

Many good questions can't be answered with the data and time available. The move to learn is from "I'm curious about X" to "I can measure Y on dataset Z, and result R would support or weaken the idea." If you can't say what result would change your mind, the question isn't ready.

### Discussion questions
- What is a question you find fascinating but probably cannot answer with the data and time you have?
- "If you can't say what result would change your mind, the question isn't ready." Do you agree? Is it always true in the humanities?

## Operationalizing

Testing an idea means turning something abstract—"this scribe is careless"—into something countable, such as "variation in sign shape per line." That translation is where rigor lives, and where bias can enter. Decide on the measure before you see the result.

### Discussion questions
- Turning "this scribe is careless" into "variation in sign shape per line" makes it measurable. What does the number miss about "careless"?
- Choosing your measure before seeing the result guards against bias. Why is that so hard to do in practice?

## Your project spec

Fill this in as completely as you can; rough answers are fine, and a partner will help sharpen them.

1. **The interest**—what are you curious about?
2. **The answerable question.**
3. **Hypothesis**—what you expect, and why.
4. **Dataset**—what data, from where, and how much.
5. **Method**—concordance, alignment, clustering, formants, entropy, and so on.
6. **The result**—what will show it?
7. **One real limitation**—what could undercut it?
