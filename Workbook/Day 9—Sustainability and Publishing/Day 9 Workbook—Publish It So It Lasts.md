## Why projects die

Think of an online tool you once relied on that no longer exists. The usual causes are the same: a server that stopped being paid for, a dependency that broke, code only one person understood. Each is avoidable.

### Discussion questions
- Think of a digital resource you relied on that disappeared. What would it have taken to keep it alive?
- Once the funding ends, whose job is it to keep a scholarly project running—the author, the institution, or the field?

## The durable pattern

Publish **static files**—HTML, an image, a data table—hosted free on GitHub Pages, with nothing that has to keep running. Then make it last:

- a **README** a stranger, including your future self, could follow,
- a **license**, so others know what they may do with it,
- data in a **documented format**,
- and, ideally, an archived snapshot for citation.

### Discussion questions
- "Static files, no server" trades flexibility for longevity. When is that trade worth making, and when isn't it?
- A README is what lets a stranger rebuild your work. What is hardest to write down because it lives only in your head?
