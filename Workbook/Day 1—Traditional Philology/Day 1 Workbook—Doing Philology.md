## The text: Beowulf, lines 1–11

*Beowulf* was composed in Old English more than a thousand years ago, and it survives in a single, fire-damaged manuscript. Here are its first eleven lines, followed by a public-domain translation.

```
Hwæt! We Gardena      in geardagum,
þeodcyninga,      þrym gefrunon,
hu ða æþelingas      ellen fremedon.
Oft Scyld Scefing      sceaþena þreatum,
monegum mægþum,      meodosetla ofteah,
egsode eorl[as].      Syððan ærest wearð
feasceaft funden,      he þæs frofre gebad,
weox under wolcnum,      weorðmyndum þah,
oðþæt him æghwylc      þara ymbsittendra
ofer hronrade      hyran scolde,
gomban gyldan.      Þæt wæs god cyning!
```

```
Lo, praise of the prowess of people-kings
of spear-armed Danes, in days long sped,
we have heard, and what honor the athelings won!
Oft Scyld the Scefing from squadroned foes,
from many a tribe, the mead-bench tore,
awing the earls. Since erst he lay
friendless, a foundling, fate repaid him:
for he waxed under welkin, in wealth he throve,
till before him the folk, both far and near,
who house by the whale-path, heard his mandate,
gave him gifts: a good king he!
```

## The first word

The poem's first word is **Hwæt!**, a call for attention with no exact modern English equivalent. Translators have rendered it many ways, and the choice sets the tone of everything after it.

| Hwæt! becomes | Effect | Translator |
|---|---|---|
| "Lo" | solemn, scriptural | Kemble / Gummere |
| "Hear me!" | a bard commanding a hall | Raffel |
| "So." | a throat cleared before a story | Heaney |
| "Listen!" | plain and direct | Liuzza |
| "Bro!" | loud, modern, competitive | Headley |

Rank them from the one you'd choose to the one you wouldn't, and say why. There's no correct answer, which is the point: a translation is an interpretation, not a copy. To choose a word here, you first have to decide what kind of poem this is.

### Discussion questions
- If every translation is an interpretation, is there any sense in which a translation can be simply wrong?
- A translator has to decide what kind of poem this is before choosing a single word. What other decisions does a translator make that a reader never sees?
- When you read a work in translation, whose voice are you hearing, the author's, the translator's, or some mixture?
- Can you think of a line whose meaning shifts completely from one translation to another?

## The life of a text

Textual criticism begins with a basic fact: we rarely have the original. For *Beowulf* the situation is extreme.

- **One copy.** The poem survives in a single manuscript, written around the year 1000. Nothing else preserves it. Everything we know about *Beowulf* depends on this one book.
- **Two scribes.** Two people copied it, and the handwriting changes partway through—different letterforms, different spelling habits, etc.
- **Fire.** In 1731 a fire damaged the library that held it. The book survived, but its edges were scorched, and over the next century the burnt margins crumbled, taking letters and whole words with them.
- **Two transcripts.** In 1786–87 a scholar named Thorkelin had the manuscript copied twice, once by a hired scribe and once by himself, after the fire but before the worst of the loss. Those two imperfect copies are now the only record of roughly 2,000 letters no longer on the page.

Each copy is a **witness**: it testifies to what the original said. When witnesses disagree, the editor weighs them and decides what the poet most likely wrote. That is textual criticism, and it's the same task in every manuscript tradition, including the ones later in this course.

## Puzzle A · Be the editor

You're preparing a new edition of *Beowulf*. At **line 6** the manuscript is damaged and hard to read. The phrase describes what King Scyld did to his enemies, and editors disagree on three readings:

1. **egsode eorl**—"terrified the nobleman," singular. Grammatically awkward; a letter has likely been lost.
2. **egsode eorlas**—"terrified the warriors," plural. Adding *-as* is the smallest change, but the line then says little. (Gummere's "awing the earls" follows it.)
3. **egsode Eorle**—"terrified the Heruli," a specific tribe. The line now makes a concrete historical claim.

Which do you adopt, and why? Reading 2 changes as little as possible; reading 3 adds information the manuscript may not support. The decision matters because every later reader inherits it as "the text."

## Puzzle B · The untranslatable

**A kenning: _hronrād_.** Old English forms compounds that name a thing indirectly. *hronrād* is *hron* (whale) + *rād* (road); it means the sea. Gummere wrote "whale-path." Another attested kenning for the sea is "the gannet's bath." Write your own English for it and defend the choice. Keeping the image gives the reader a small riddle; giving the plain meaning loses the figure. You usually can't do both, and it's worth naming what your choice costs.

**A word with no English equivalent: _wyrd_.** *wyrd* is usually translated "fate," but it isn't the fixed fate of Greek tragedy. It's a more open sense of how events unfold, one a decisive act can sometimes turn. (Gummere even adds it to line 7 as "fate repaid him," where the Old English has no such word.) Define *wyrd* in a sentence, choose a single English word for it, then state what that word leaves out. Some words carry a whole framework the target language has no slot for.

## Where words come from

One last point. Set *Beowulf*'s *fæder* beside English *father*, German *Vater*, and Latin *pater*, and a regularity appears: where Latin has *p*, the Germanic words have *f*. In the nineteenth century scholars showed that such correspondences are systematic rather than accidental—the sound shift now known as Grimm's Law. Comparing old texts to reconstruct the history of a language is what turned philology into linguistics. The notebook lets you check the pattern yourself.

### Discussion questions
- If sound change is regular, what can it let us reconstruct about languages that left no records?
- Philology stays with the particular text; linguistics moves to language as a system. What does each gain, and what does each give up?
