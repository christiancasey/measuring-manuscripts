## How do we know Chaucer rhymed?

Yesterday's text already left clues. Chaucer writes in couplets, in a steady ten-beat line, but the rhythm only works if you pronounce things we have since dropped. The opening keeps its beat only when you sound the final *-e* in *soote* (say it *SOH-tuh*) and give *Aprille* its full three syllables. The meter records a pronunciation we no longer use.

Rhyme records more. Some of Chaucer's rhymes no longer rhyme for us, because the vowels of English shifted in the centuries after him, in the change called the Great Vowel Shift. Where a rhyme has broken on the page, it tells us the two words once sounded alike, and roughly how.

So the text carries evidence about its own sound. To use that evidence, though, we need a way to talk about sound precisely. That's what phonetics gives us.

## A crash course in phonetics

Phonetics is the study of speech sounds as physical events: how the body makes them, and what they look like as sound. None of it needs prior training. You already do all of it every time you talk. What follows names the parts and the categories, so that when we start measuring we have words for what we are measuring.

### Speech is waves in air

Every speech sound starts as a column of air leaving the lungs. On its way out it passes through three stations, and each one does something to it.

First the *lungs* push the air up. They are the power supply. On their own they make no sound, only a stream.

Then the air reaches the *larynx*, the box of cartilage you can feel as your "Adam's apple." Stretched across it are the *vocal folds* (older books say "vocal cords"), two flaps of tissue. They can do two things. Held apart, they let the air pass silently, as during the *s* of *sip*. Drawn together and set vibrating by the airstream, they buzz, opening and closing hundreds of times a second. This is *voicing*, the raw sound behind every vowel and behind consonants like *z*. The rate of that buzz is the *pitch* of the voice: faster buzz, higher pitch.

Above the larynx is the *supralaryngeal vocal tract*, everything from the larynx up to the lips and nostrils. This is the chamber that shapes the buzz into recognizable sounds. The parts that do the shaping are the *articulators*:

- the *lips*
- the *teeth*
- the *alveolar ridge*, the bony shelf just behind the upper teeth (run your tongue tip up from your teeth and you feel it)
- the *hard palate*, the dome at the roof of the mouth
- the *soft palate* or *velum*, the soft tissue further back
- the *uvula*, the small flap hanging at the very back
- the *tongue*, which we divide into *tip*, *body*, and *root*, because different parts of it act independently
- the *glottis*, the space between the vocal folds themselves.

Some articulators move (the tongue, the lips, the velum). Others are fixed targets the movers reach toward (the teeth, the alveolar ridge, the hard palate). A speech sound is largely a description of which mover went to which target, and what it did there.

### Consonants: three parameters

A consonant obstructs the airstream somewhere in the tract. We describe each one with three facts: *where* the obstruction is (place), *how* the air gets past it (manner), and **whether the vocal folds are buzzing** (voicing). Give those three and you have named the consonant.

*Place of articulation* is where in the tract the action happens. Working front to back:

- *bilabial*: both lips, as in *p*, *b*, *m* (*pat*, *bat*, *mat*)
- *labiodental*: lower lip against upper teeth, as in *f*, *v* (*fan*, *van*)
- *dental*: tongue at the teeth, as in the two *th* sounds (*thin*, *then*)
- *alveolar*: tongue at the ridge behind the teeth, as in *t*, *d*, *s*, *z*, *n*, *l* (*tip*, *dip*, *sip*, *zip*, *nip*, *lip*)
- *postalveolar*: just behind that ridge, as in *sh* and the middle of *vision* (*ship*, *measure*)
- *palatal*: tongue body at the hard palate, as in *y* (*yes*)
- *velar*: tongue body at the soft palate, as in *k*, *g*, and *ng* (*kit*, *get*, *sing*)
- *uvular*: back of the tongue at the uvula. English has none, but it is the French *r* in *rouge* and the Arabic *q*
- *pharyngeal*: a constriction in the throat itself. English has none, but Arabic does
- *glottal*: at the vocal folds, as in *h* (*hat*) and the catch in the middle of *uh-oh*.

*Manner of articulation* is how completely, and in what way, the air is obstructed:

- *plosive* (or *stop*): the tract is sealed, pressure builds, then releases in a little burst: *p, b, t, d, k, g* (*pat, get*)
- *nasal*: the mouth is sealed but the velum drops so air escapes through the nose: *m, n, ng* (*mat, nap, sing*). Pinch your nose and try to say "mmm". It dies, because the only exit is blocked
- *fricative*: the tract is narrowed but not sealed, so the air hisses through: *f, v, s, z, sh, th, h* (*fan, sip, ship*)
- *affricate*: a stop released into a fricative, as one sound: the *ch* of *church* and the *j* of *judge*
- *trill*: an articulator flutters in the airstream: the rolled Spanish or Italian *r*
- *tap* or *flap*: a single quick contact: the American *t* in *butter*, which is really a flap
- *approximant*: the tract narrows but not enough to make noise: *w, y, r* (*wet, yet, red*)
- *lateral*: air flows around the sides of the tongue: *l* (*let*).

*Voicing* is the third parameter, and it is the simplest to feel. Put a finger on your throat, against the larynx, and hold a long "sssss." Now switch to "zzzzz" without stopping. You feel the buzz switch on. *s* and *z* are made in exactly the same place, in exactly the same manner. The only difference is that the vocal folds are vibrating for *z* and still for *s*. The same pairing runs all through English: *p/b*, *t/d*, *k/g*, *f/v* are each a voiceless sound and its voiced twin. Vowels are all voiced.

Almost every sound here is made on air pushed out of the lungs. These are *pulmonic* sounds, and they are nearly all you need for English or Coptic or Arabic. A few languages also use *non-pulmonic* sounds made with other airstreams (the clicks of southern African languages, for instance), but we can set those aside.

### Vowels in depth

A vowel is the opposite of a consonant: the tract is open, the air flows freely, and what changes from one vowel to the next is the *shape* of the open tube, mostly where the tongue body sits and what the lips do. Because nothing actually touches, you can't describe a vowel by "place" and "manner." Instead phoneticians use a small set of continuous dimensions.

*Height* is how high the tongue body rides in the mouth. Say *beet*, then *bat*, slowly, and feel your jaw and tongue drop. *beet* is a *close* (or *high*) vowel, while *bat* is an *open* (or *low*) vowel. Between them are intermediate steps: *close-mid* (the vowel of *bait*) and *open-mid* (the vowel of *bet*).

*Backness* is how far forward or back the tongue body sits. Say *beet*, then *boot*. The tongue pulls back for *boot*. *beet* is a *front* vowel, *boot* is a *back* vowel, and the vowel in the middle of *sofa* (the relaxed "uh" called *schwa*) sits in the *central* middle.

*Rounding* is what the lips do. Say *boot* and watch a mirror or feel your lips: they purse into a circle. Now say *beet*: they spread. *boot* is *rounded*, *beet* is *unrounded*. In English the back vowels happen to be rounded and the front ones not, but the two are independent. French *tu* is a front vowel said with rounded lips, which is why it is hard for English speakers.

Phoneticians arrange these on the *vowel quadrilateral*, a four-sided figure that stands for the space inside the mouth: front at the left, back at the right, high at the top, low at the bottom. Each vowel is a point in it. To anchor the chart against something universal rather than one language's vowels, phoneticians defined a set of *cardinal vowels*: reference points at the extreme corners and edges of the possible space, like the cardinal directions on a compass. Any real vowel can then be placed relative to those fixed points.

One more distinction English leans on heavily: *tense* versus *lax*. The vowel of *beet* is tense. The vowel of *bit* is lax: shorter, slightly lower and more central, the tongue less strained. *beet/bit*, *bait/bet*, *boot/book* are tense/lax pairs. The difference is easy to hear and surprisingly hard to draw, which is part of why English vowels resist tidy spelling.

### Beyond single sounds: suprasegmentals

So far we have described sounds one at a time, the *segments*. But speech also carries information riding *across* segments, in properties of whole syllables and phrases. These are the *suprasegmentals*, and they matter for reading old verse.

*Stress* is the prominence of one syllable over its neighbors: louder, longer, higher. English uses it to distinguish words: *RECord* the noun versus *recORD* the verb, *PERmit* versus *perMIT*. It is also what meter is built from: Chaucer's line is a pattern of stressed and unstressed syllables, and a line scans only when the stresses fall where the pattern wants them.

*Vowel length* is how long a vowel is held. In some languages a long vowel and a short vowel are different sounds entirely. Chaucer's "long" vowels were genuinely longer, and the Great Vowel Shift is a story about what happened to them.

*Tone* is the use of pitch to distinguish words: in Mandarin, the syllable *ma* means different things on a high, rising, dipping, or falling pitch. English has no lexical tone, but it has *intonation*, the rise and fall of pitch across a phrase that marks a question, a statement, or surprise. "You're leaving." and "You're leaving?" differ only in intonation.

*Syllable structure* is how sounds group into syllables, each built around a vowel (the nucleus) with optional consonants before (the onset) and after (the coda). *strengths* is one English syllable with a heavy onset and coda, while *a* is one with neither. How many syllables a word has, and where they fall, is exactly what meter counts, which is why giving *Aprille* its full three syllables matters.

### Phonology: which differences count

Phonetics describes the sounds the mouth can make. *Phonology* asks which of those differences a particular language actually *uses* to tell words apart. The two are different questions, and the gap between them is where a lot of the subtlety lives.

A *phoneme* is a sound difference that changes meaning in a given language. In English, /p/ and /b/ are separate phonemes, because *pat* and *bat* are different words. We show that with a *minimal pair*: two words that differ in exactly one sound and mean different things. *pat/bat*, *sip/zip*, *beet/bit*: each pair proves its two sounds are distinct phonemes in English.

But a single phoneme is pronounced differently in different places, and English speakers don't notice. The /p/ of *pin* comes with a puff of air (hold a hand to your mouth and feel it). The /p/ of *spin* does not. These two actual sounds are *allophones*: physically different realizations of the same phoneme. We never hear them as different because no English word is distinguished by the puff. The difference is automatic, governed by where the sound sits. When two sounds never appear in the same context (aspirated *p* only at the start of a stressed syllable, plain *p* only after *s*) they are in *complementary distribution*, a sure sign they are allophones of one phoneme rather than two phonemes.

Phonemes are written in slashes, /p/. Specific sounds in brackets, [pʰ] for the aspirated one. So the English phoneme /p/ has allophones [pʰ] and [p]. The same physical sound can be a phoneme in one language and a mere allophone in another. Aspiration *is* contrastive in Hindi, where it makes minimal pairs.

Two more facts about sounds in sequence, because they bear on reading scribes:

*Coarticulation* is the way neighboring sounds bleed into each other. You don't say a word as a string of separate sounds. The tongue is already moving toward the next target while finishing the last. The /k/ of *key* is made further forward than the /k/ of *cool*, pulled there by the front vowel that follows. Speech is smeared, not beaded.

*Assimilation* is coarticulation gone far enough to change the category. *in-* plus *possible* becomes *impossible*: the /n/ turns into /m/ to match the following lip sound. English *handbag* is usually said "hambag." When a scribe writes what assimilation produced rather than the "underlying" form, that spelling is evidence about how the word was actually said.

## Vowels as two numbers

Now we can connect the open shape of a vowel to a number we can measure off a recording. The link is the *source–filter theory*, and it is exactly what the notebook's synthesizer acts out.

A voiced sound has two parts. The *source* is the buzz from the vocal folds, a raw, pitched rasp, the same for every vowel. The *filter* is the tube of the vocal tract above the larynx, and its shape decides which frequencies in that buzz get amplified and which get damped. Change the shape of the tube, move the tongue, round the lips, and you change which frequencies ring. A vowel is one buzz played through differently shaped tubes.

Every tube has *resonances*, frequencies at which it rings strongly, the way a bottle has a pitch when you blow across it. The vocal tract's resonances are called *formants*, and they show up as peaks of energy in the sound. The vowel you hear is set almost entirely by where the lowest formants sit. Crucially, the formants depend on the *shape* of the tract, not on the pitch of the buzz: you can say *ah* high or low and it stays *ah*, because moving the pitch moves the source without moving the filter. Pitch and vowel are independent, and the notebook shows this directly.


The two lowest formants are enough to tell most vowels apart, and they line up with two of the dimensions we just learned:

- the first formant, **F1**, tracks tongue *height*, but inversely. A high (close) tongue gives a *low* F1. A low (open) tongue gives a *high* F1. *beet* (high tongue) has a low F1, while *bat* (low tongue) has a high F1.
- the second formant, **F2**, tracks *backness*. A front tongue gives a high F2. A back tongue gives a low F2. *beet* (front) has a high F2, while *boot* (back) has a low F2.

So each vowel becomes a pair of numbers, (F1, F2). Plot F1 against F2 and every vowel is a point. A speaker's whole set of vowels forms a cloud called the *vowel space*. If you flip both axes, so high F1 points down and high F2 points left, the cloud comes out in the same arrangement as the vowel quadrilateral drawn from the mouth. We will have reconstructed the chart of tongue positions from sound alone, without ever looking inside a mouth.

Different speakers and dialects shift and stretch the cloud, and that is exactly what makes it useful: the shape and placement of someone's vowel space is a fingerprint of how they speak. The notebook synthesizes vowels with chosen formants, listens to them, measures the formants back, and draws the space.

## The Great Vowel Shift

Now we can say precisely what changed between Chaucer and us, because the change was a movement through exactly this space. Over roughly 1400–1700 the long vowels of English all shifted, and they shifted in a chain.

Chaucer's long vowels had their "continental" values, the ones the letters still carry in most European languages. His ⟨i⟩ in *ryde* sounded like our *ee*. His ⟨ou⟩ in *hous* sounded like our *oo*. His long ⟨a⟩ in *name* was *ah*. And the ⟨oo⟩ of *soote* and *roote*, two words from the very first lines, was a long *oh*, not our *oo*. Read the opening with those values and the rhymes lock back into place.

Then every long vowel rose, each climbing into the slot above it. The two already at the top, *ee* and *oo*, had nowhere higher to go, so they broke apart into diphthongs: Chaucer's *ryde* (*reede*) became *ride*, his *hous* (*hoose*) became *house*. Spelling, meanwhile, had just been frozen in place by the printing press, which is why English vowels are still spelled for a pronunciation we abandoned four centuries ago.

**Why did it happen?** Nobody is sure. The shift is far better documented than explained. The mechanism is usually drawn as one of two shapes:

- a *pull chain*: the top vowels diphthongized first, opening a gap that pulled the vowel beneath up to fill it, and so on down the line
- a *push chain*: a lower vowel began rising first and crowded the one above it, shoving the whole series upward until the top vowels were forced out into diphthongs.


Same result, opposite causality. The question is only which vowel moved first. The triggers people propose for that first move (dialect contact after the plague, prestige imitation, the pull of French) are educated guesses. The notebook draws the whole chain as arrows around the vowel space, where the circular motion is easy to see.

## From living sound to a dead language

Coptic, the last stage of Egyptian, was written in letters borrowed from Greek, and no one knows how those letters map onto real spoken vowels. We read the consonants with some confidence, relatively speaking. But there's a second, stranger window: a medieval manuscript that writes *Arabic* (a living language) in *Coptic* letters. It's a copy of the *Sayings of the Desert Fathers*, made when Coptic was fading but still read, by someone fluent enough in the script to spell another language with it. A living language we can still hear, written in the script we're trying to decode. In miniature, it's a Rosetta Stone for Coptic pronunciation.

The tempting way to read it is by eye: find a word, see which Coptic letter sits over which Arabic sound, declare a result. The whole point of the method is that this is exactly how you fool yourself. Choose your examples and you can "prove" almost any pronunciation you like. The evidence will oblige, and your own expectations will pick the examples for you. So the method refuses the eye. A program aligns the whole text, Coptic letter against Arabic letter, and measures how strongly each pair travels together (a correlation called the phi coefficient).

The dull results are the proof it works: the letters everyone already agrees on, ⟨ⲗ⟩, ⟨ⲙ⟩, ⟨ⲛ⟩ for *l, m, n*, come out firmly paired, as they must. The interesting results are the payoff, the cases where a letter behaves differently than the handbooks assume. ⟨ⲅ⟩, normally read *g*, lines up with [ɣ]. ⟨ⲃ⟩, normally *b*, lines up with [w]. And there's a fresh possibility for the sound of ⟨ϫ⟩. The same machine that confirms the obvious is the one you then trust on the doubtful. The notebook runs a small version of that measurement and draws the correlation table as a colormap.

The consonants are done. The vowels are the harder half, and where this morning's tools come back. Measure the vowels of the living Arabic dialects with the formant code you already have, line them against the Coptic spellings, and reason toward sounds no one has heard in a thousand years. That part is still to come.

The reasoning leans on assumptions, and the discipline is in stating them out loud: that the living language keeps the relevant distinctions, that the spelling is shallow enough to trust, that the scribe heard what we think he heard. None of it is certain. All of it is more than we had.
