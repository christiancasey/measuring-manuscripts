## Phone vs. phoneme

Say "key" and "caw." The initial sound differs physically, but English treats both as /k/, and the difference never distinguishes one word from another. A **phone** is a sound as produced; a **phoneme** is a sound as it functions to separate words. Writing systems generally record phonemes, which is why spelling can reveal the sound system behind it.

### Discussion questions
- English hears the two "k" sounds in "key" and "caw" as the same. What does it cost a speaker not to hear a difference their language ignores?
- If spelling records phonemes rather than phones, what can a writing system never tell us about how a language actually sounded?
- Can you think of two sounds your own language treats as identical that another language keeps apart?

## Vowels as two numbers

A vowel is well described by two measurements: **F1**, which tracks how high the tongue sits, and **F2**, which tracks how far forward it is. Plot F1 against F2 and each vowel becomes a point; a speaker's vowels form a cloud. Different speakers and dialects shift the cloud.

## Record and measure

The notebook runs its first half on published average values, with no audio required. Then:

- Record yourself saying the vowels in "beet, bat, boot" (or the target set).
- Extract F1 and F2 for each and plot your vowel space.
- Compare your cloud with a partner's. Where they differ, is it dialect?

## The bridge

This is the move that matters. When a language is written in a borrowed alphabet—Arabic in Coptic letters, for instance—living dialects together with that spelling let you reason about sounds no one has heard in fifteen hundred years. The reasoning depends on assumptions you have to state plainly. Stating them is the method, not a footnote to it.
